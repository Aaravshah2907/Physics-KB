# Maxwell's Equations

## Summary
Maxwell's equations represent a set of four partial differential equations that form the foundation of classical electromagnetism, classical optics, and electric circuits. They describe how electric and magnetic fields are generated by charges, currents, and by changes of each other. The equations provide a mathematical framework for the unification of electricity and magnetism into a single force: electromagnetism.

## The Four Fundamental Equations

The equations are most commonly expressed in two equivalent forms: **differential** (local) and **integral** (global). The differential form describes the fields at a specific point in space, while the integral form relates the fields to the total charge and current within a volume or across a surface.

### 1. [[Gauss's Law]] (Electric)
This law relates the net electric flux through a closed surface to the net internal charge.

*   **Differential Form**: $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}$$
*   **Integral Form**: $$\oint_{\partial V} \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{a} = \frac{Q_{enc}}{\epsilon_0}$$

It implies that electric field lines originate from positive charges and terminate on negative charges. For a detailed derivation and applications, see [[gauss-law]].

### 2. [[Gauss's Law]] for Magnetism
This law states that there are no "magnetic charges" (monopoles); the total magnetic flux through a closed surface is always zero.

*   **Differential Form**: $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$$
*   **Integral Form**: $$\oint_{\partial V} \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{a} = 0$$

Physical Intuition: Magnetic field lines are continuous loops without start or end points.

### 3. Faraday's Law of Induction
This law describes how a time-varying magnetic field induces an electric field (the principle behind electric generators).

*   **Differential Form**: $$\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}$$
*   **Integral Form**: $$\oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

Where $\Phi_B$ is the magnetic flux. The negative sign represents Lenz's Law, indicating that the induced field opposes the change in flux.

![Faraday Induction](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d4/Faradays_law_of_induction.svg)

### 4. Ampère-Maxwell Law
This law relates magnetic fields to electric currents and changing electric fields.

*   **Differential Form**: $$\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \left( \mathbf{J} + \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} \right)$$
*   **Integral Form**: $$\oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I_{enc} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt}$$

The term $\epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}$ is known as the **displacement current**, Maxwell's critical contribution to the theory.

## The Displacement Current

### Mathematical Necessity
Before Maxwell, Ampère's law was simply $\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J}$. However, taking the divergence of both sides yields:
$$\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) = \mu_0 (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J})$$
Since the divergence of a curl is always zero ($\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) \equiv 0$), this implies $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0$. This contradicts the **Continuity Equation**, which states:
$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = -\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}$$
Maxwell resolved this by adding the term $\epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}$, ensuring that:
$$\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) = \mu_0 \left( \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} + \epsilon_0 \nabla \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} \right) = \mu_0 \left( -\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial (\epsilon_0 \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E})}{\partial t} \right)$$
Using [[Gauss's Law]] ($\epsilon_0 \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \rho$), the terms in the parentheses cancel out to zero, making the equation mathematically consistent with the conservation of charge.

![Displacement Current in a Capacitor](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Displacement_current_in_a_capacitor.svg/1024px-Displacement_current_in_a_capacitor.svg.png)

## Maxwell's Equations in Matter
In a medium, the equations are often written in terms of the auxiliary fields $\mathbf{D}$ (Electric Displacement) and $\mathbf{H}$ (Magnetic Intensity) to account for bound charges and currents.

1.  $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{D} = \rho_f$
2.  $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$
3.  $\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}$
4.  $\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J}_f + \frac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t}$

Where:
*   $\mathbf{D} = \epsilon_0 \mathbf{E} + \mathbf{P}$
*   $\mathbf{H} = \frac{1}{\mu_0} \mathbf{B} - \mathbf{M}$

## Electromagnetic Potentials
To simplify solving Maxwell's equations, we often define fields in terms of potentials.

### Scalar and Vector Potentials
Since $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$, we can define the **Magnetic Vector Potential** $\mathbf{A}$ such that:
$$\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$$
Substituting this into Faraday's Law:
$$\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) \implies \nabla \times \left( \mathbf{E} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{A}}{\partial t} \right) = 0$$
This allows us to define the **Electric Scalar Potential** $\phi$ such that:
$$\mathbf{E} = -\nabla \phi - \frac{\partial \mathbf{A}}{\partial t}$$

### Gauge Transformations
The potentials are not unique. We can transform them without changing the physical $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ fields:
$$\mathbf{A}' = \mathbf{A} + \nabla \Lambda$$
$$\phi' = \phi - \frac{\partial \Lambda}{\partial t}$$
Common choices of $\Lambda$ include the **Coulomb Gauge** ($\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} = 0$) and the **Lorenz Gauge** ($\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t} = 0$).

## Derivation of the [[electromagnetic-waves]] Equation
In a vacuum ($\rho = 0, \mathbf{J} = 0$), Maxwell's equations lead to the wave equation. Taking the curl of Faraday's Law:
$$\nabla \times (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) = -\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla \times \mathbf{B})$$
Using the vector identity $\nabla \times (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) = \nabla(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}) - \nabla^2 \mathbf{E}$ and substituting the Ampère-Maxwell law for $\nabla \times \mathbf{B}$:
$$\nabla(0) - \nabla^2 \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left( \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} \right)$$
$$\nabla^2 \mathbf{E} = \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}$$
This is the standard form of the 3D wave equation, where the speed of light $c$ is:
$$c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}} \approx 3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$$

## Lorentz Force Law
While not one of Maxwell's equations, the **Lorentz Force Law** is required to describe how the fields interact with matter:
$$\mathbf{F} = q(\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B})$$
